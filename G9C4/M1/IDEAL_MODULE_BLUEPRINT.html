G9C4M1 — Learn
Recognise that some situations have uncertain outcomes, understand that this uncertainty is called chance, and see that chance varies from situation to situation.
CPA Progression

Abstract none
This is a fully Concrete-phase module — the primary CPA phase is concrete throughout. The chance strip is a physical/on-screen manipulable object (concrete representation). No numerical or symbolic abstraction is introduced. The module deliberately stays at the concrete qualitative level to establish the conceptual foundation before M2 (pictorial chance line with more/less/equally likely) and M8 (abstract numerical probability). All slides use concrete event cards, physical bag imagery, and qualitative strip labels. No transition to pictorial or abstract is warranted in this module.

Convention Requirements (4)
chance vs probability
Use 'chance' consistently throughout this module. Do not introduce 'probability' as a term on any student-facing slide or applet screen. If students raise the term 'probability', teacher note should acknowledge it and redirect: 'The precise numerical meaning of probability will be explored in a later module.' The Math_Vault (Slide 4) must define 'chance' and 'uncertain outcome' without referencing 'probability'. All feedback strings in applets must use 'chance', not 'probability'.
Slide 4
certain vs impossible as boundary cases
Establish 'certain' (always happens, no exceptions) and 'impossible' (can never happen, no exceptions) as hard boundaries of the chance spectrum in the Math_Vault (Slide 4). Use unambiguous Indonesian examples: 'The sun rises in the east tomorrow' (certain); 'You draw a green marble from a bag containing only red and blue marbles' (impossible). Slides must not allow students to conflate 'very likely' with 'certain' or 'very unlikely' with 'impossible'.
Slide 4
uncertain outcome vs unpredictable outcome
Clarify in the Math_Vault (Slide 4) and reinforce on Slide 3 that 'uncertain' means we cannot be completely sure of the result before it happens — but we can still describe and compare how likely different outcomes are. Use the phrase 'we cannot be certain, but we can still say which is more likely' on at least one student-facing slide. Applet feedback strings must not imply that uncertain events are unknowable or incomparable.
Slide 3
Indonesian example contexts
Prioritise Indonesian everyday contexts for all event examples: weather in specific Indonesian cities (Jakarta, Bandung, Surabaya), Indonesian national scenarios (Indonesia 2045), drawing items from locally recognisable contexts. Coin and die examples are acceptable as secondary illustrations only (used in Exploration 4.2 game contexts). All named characters must use Indonesian names (e.g., Pak Budi, Bu Sari). Generic Western examples must not appear as primary illustrations.
