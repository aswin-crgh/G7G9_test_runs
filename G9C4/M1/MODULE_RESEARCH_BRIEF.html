Misconception Inventory (3)
#280 — Students treat chance/likelihood as a vague linguistic label ('probably', 'maybe', 'unlikely') rather than something that can be compared or ordered with precision. At this introductory module, the risk is that students leave with only informal language and no sense that chance is a measurable, comparable quantity — resisting the idea that some uncertain events are more likely than others in a way that can be stated clearly.
Surfacing strategy
Present two contrasting uncertain events — e.g., 'Will it rain in Jakarta in December?' vs 'Will you roll a 6 on a die?' — and ask students to say which has more chance. If students respond only with 'both are uncertain' or 'I don't know', the misconception is active. A Shout_Out_Loud prompt asking students to rank three events by chance surfaces this without requiring numerical precision.

#281 — Students believe that all uncertain events are equally likely — if something can happen or not happen, they assign 50/50 chance by default. At this module's concrete scope, this surfaces as students claiming 'it might happen or it might not, so it's the same chance' for events with clearly different likelihoods (e.g., drawing a red marble from a bag of 9 red and 1 blue vs drawing the blue one).
Surfacing strategy
Present a bag with 9 red marbles and 1 blue marble. Ask: 'Is the chance of drawing red the same as drawing blue?' Students who say 'yes, both are uncertain' reveal the equally-likely default. Use a Comparative Analysis interaction — show two bags with very different compositions and ask students to compare chances. The contrast makes the inequality of chance visible without requiring calculation.

#283 — Students treat all probabilities between the extremes (impossible and certain) as vague and meaningless — they can identify impossible and certain events but cannot reason about relative likelihood of uncertain events. At this module's scope, this means students accept that 'high chance' and 'low chance' are different in words but cannot order or compare events by their chance.
Surfacing strategy
Present three events with clearly different likelihoods (e.g., 'the sun rises tomorrow' — certain; 'you draw a red card from a standard deck' — roughly equal; 'you roll a 6 three times in a row' — low) and ask students to arrange them from least to most chance. Students who can only classify the extremes but lump the middle events together reveal this misconception. A Sequencing & Ordering interaction with 4–5 events on a chance strip surfaces the gap.

IAS Patterns (3)
Sequencing & Ordering — Chance Strip Placement
Cognitive actions: Students are given 4–6 event cards drawn from Indonesian everyday contexts (e.g., 'It rains in Bandung in January', 'You draw a red card from a full deck', 'A coin lands on heads', 'You roll a 6 three times in a row', 'The sun rises tomorrow'). They drag and drop the cards onto a horizontal chance strip labelled Impossible → Certain, then compare their ordering with a partner. The interaction surfaces whether students treat all uncertain events as equivalent (M281, M283) or can discriminate degrees of chance (M280).
When to use: Core interaction for this module — used after the Math_Vault defines 'chance' and 'uncertain outcome', and before the Exit_Question. Also serves as the Shout_Out_Loud prompt vehicle: students justify their ordering aloud before seeing the model answer.
Comparative Analysis — Two-Bag Contrast
Cognitive actions: Two bags are shown side by side: Bag A has 9 red marbles and 1 blue; Bag B has 5 red and 5 blue. Students answer: 'From which bag is drawing red more likely?' and 'Are both draws uncertain?' This forces the distinction between 'uncertain' (both are) and 'equally likely' (they are not), directly targeting M281. No calculation required — the visual contrast of counts is sufficient at concrete phase.
When to use: Immediately after the Snapshot introducing uncertain outcomes, before the Math_Vault. Surfaces M281 (equally-likely default) as a productive conflict before the definition of chance is formalised.
Debate & Justification — Always / Sometimes / Never Certain
Cognitive actions: Students are presented with statements such as: 'If an event is uncertain, we cannot say anything about its chance.' They classify each as Always True / Sometimes True / Never True and write a one-sentence justification. This consolidates the distinction between uncertainty (we cannot predict with certainty) and chance (we can still compare and describe likelihood). Targets M280 and M283.
When to use: Consolidation phase — after the Sequencing & Ordering interaction and before the Exit_Question. Suitable for the Shout_Out_Loud trope slot.
Convention Flags (4)
chance vs probability
Risk: This module deliberately uses 'chance' rather than 'probability' to delay the formal numerical term until M8. Students and teachers familiar with the formal term may introduce 'probability' prematurely, undermining the scaffolding design. The false-friend risk (KB false_friend id 155) is high: students use 'probability' loosely in everyday speech.
Mitigation: Use 'chance' consistently throughout this module. Do not introduce 'probability' as a term — if students raise it, acknowledge it and note that the precise numerical meaning will be explored in a later module. The Math_Vault should define 'chance' and 'uncertain outcome' without referencing 'probability'.
certain vs impossible as boundary cases
Risk: Students may treat 'certain' and 'impossible' as part of the uncertain spectrum rather than as its boundaries. Conflating 'very likely' with 'certain' or 'very unlikely' with 'impossible' is common at this introductory level.
Mitigation: Establish 'certain' (always happens, no exceptions) and 'impossible' (can never happen, no exceptions) as hard boundaries in the Math_Vault. Use Indonesian examples where the boundary is unambiguous: 'The sun rises in the east tomorrow' (certain); 'You draw a green marble from a bag containing only red and blue marbles' (impossible).
uncertain outcome vs unpredictable outcome
Risk: Students may interpret 'uncertain' as meaning 'completely random and unknowable', conflating uncertainty with ignorance. This can lead to the belief that no comparison of chance is possible for uncertain events (M280, M283).
Mitigation: Clarify in the Math_Vault that 'uncertain' means we cannot be completely sure of the result before it happens — but we can still describe and compare how likely different outcomes are. Use the phrase 'we cannot be certain, but we can still say which is more likely'.
Indonesian example contexts
Risk: Generic examples (coin flips, dice) are culturally neutral but miss the Kurikulum Merdeka expectation of Indonesian-contextualised learning. The textbook uses Indonesia's HDI and 2045 development goals as a probability context (p.216).
Mitigation: Prioritise Indonesian everyday contexts for event examples: weather in specific Indonesian cities, drawing items from a warung, outcomes of traditional games (e.g., gasing), or national development scenarios as used in Exploration 4.1. Coin and die examples are acceptable as secondary illustrations only.

Synthesis Narrative
This module is the conceptual gateway to the entire probability chapter, and its instructional challenge is deceptively simple: students already have rich everyday language for uncertainty ('maybe', 'probably', 'no chance') but this language is imprecise and, crucially, flat — it does not naturally encode the idea that chance varies in degree across situations. The three misconceptions linked to this LO (M280, M281, M283) all share a common root: students treat uncertainty as a binary state (certain vs not certain) rather than as a continuous quality that can be compared and ordered. The module's job is not to introduce numbers — that belongs to M8 — but to establish that chance is a real, comparable property of situations, that some uncertain events have more chance than others, and that the words 'impossible' and 'certain' mark the boundaries of a spectrum. The chance strip (a qualitative, label-only version of VIR 3's probability number line) is the single most important visual model for this module. Positioned at the concrete CPA phase, it gives students a physical or on-screen object to manipulate — placing event cards from Indonesian everyday life onto a spectrum from Impossible to Certain. This manipulation directly surfaces M281 (the equally-likely default): when students place 'drawing red from a bag of 9 red and 1 blue' and 'drawing red from a bag of 5 red and 5 blue' at the same position on the strip, the conflict is visible and discussable without any calculation. The Comparative Analysis — Two-Bag Contrast interaction is the productive-conflict entry point that creates the cognitive need for the chance strip before the Math_Vault formalises the vocabulary. The Sequencing & Ordering — Chance Strip Placement interaction then consolidates the concept by requiring students to rank multiple events, surfacing M283 (inability to order events between the extremes) as a gap that the strip's spatial structure helps resolve. The module's trope sequence should follow this logic: a Snapshot visual anchor establishes the concrete scenario (uncertain vs certain vs impossible events using Indonesian contexts from Exploration 4.1 — Indonesia's 2045 development goals, weather in specific cities); the Math_Vault defines 'chance' and 'uncertain outcome' precisely, with 'certain' and 'impossible' as hard boundaries; the Shout_Out_Loud prompt asks students to justify their chance-strip ordering aloud, surfacing M280 (resistance to comparing chance across uncertain events); and the Exit_Question tests whether students can correctly identify which of two situations has more chance without numerical calculation. The false-friend risk around 'probability' (KB false_friend id 155) is managed by a strict convention: this module uses 'chance' throughout, and the Math_Vault explicitly notes that a more precise numerical measure will be introduced later. The parent module should NOT include a Math_Trap trope here — Math_Trap is appropriate for M280 at the numerical level (M8 scope), but at this introductory concrete phase, productive conflict through Comparative Analysis is the right vehicle. The chapter research confirms that M280's numerical surfacing strategy belongs to M8, not M1.
