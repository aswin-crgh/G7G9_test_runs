Misconception Inventory (3)
#280 — Students treat chance as a vague linguistic label ('probably', 'maybe', 'unlikely') and resist comparing uncertain events with any precision. In this applet's binary-sort context, the risk is that students drag cards arbitrarily — treating HIGH and LOW as interchangeable labels — rather than reasoning about which situations genuinely have more chance. The applet surfaces this when students place events like 'rain in Bogor in January' in LOW Chance or 'helicopter landing at school tomorrow' in HIGH Chance without any reasoning anchor.
Surfacing strategy
Design the feedback flow so that when a card is placed incorrectly, the system does NOT simply say 'wrong' — instead it prompts: 'Think about how often this usually happens. Does it happen most of the time, or almost never?' This forces the student to reason from frequency rather than from vague feeling. The Repair-Guided Feedback archetype (IAS id 34) is the correct vehicle.

#281 — Students believe all uncertain events are equally likely — if something can happen or not happen, they default to 50/50. In a binary HIGH/LOW sort, this manifests as students placing all uncertain events in the same category (typically HIGH, since 'it could happen') or expressing that the sort is arbitrary because 'both could happen'. This is the highest-severity misconception for this applet and the primary reason the binary sort interaction was designed.
Surfacing strategy
Include at least two event-card pairs in the sort set where one event is clearly HIGH and the other clearly LOW, drawn from the same domain (e.g., 'It will rain in Bogor tomorrow in January' vs 'A helicopter will land at your school tomorrow'). When a student places both in the same category, the feedback should contrast the two: 'Both are uncertain — but are they equally likely? Think about how often each one actually happens.' The Contrastive Feedback archetype (IAS id 32) is the correct vehicle for this pair-level feedback.

#283 — Students can identify impossible and certain events but treat all uncertain events as vague and undifferentiated — they cannot reason about relative likelihood between uncertain events. In the binary sort, this surfaces as random placement of uncertain events, or as students correctly placing the extreme cases (if any appear) but failing on the middle-range events. The applet's binary sort is a simplified version of the full ordering task — it reduces the cognitive demand to a two-category decision, which is the appropriate scaffold at this concrete phase.
Surfacing strategy
Include 2–3 event cards that are 'borderline' (e.g., 'You will eat rice for lunch today' — HIGH for most Indonesian students; 'You will score 100% on your next test' — LOW but not impossible). These borderline cards force students to reason rather than rely on extreme-case recognition. Feedback for borderline cards should acknowledge the reasoning challenge: 'This one is tricky! Think about how often this happens for most students your age.' The Repair-Guided Feedback archetype (IAS id 34) handles this.

Visual Model Recommendations (1)
VIR 3 — Binary Chance Sort — Two-Zone Drop Area (HIGH Chance / LOW Chance)
Rationale
The applet's primary visual structure: two clearly labelled drop zones (HIGH Chance in green, LOW Chance in orange/red) with a stack of draggable event cards. Students drag each card to the zone they judge correct. This is a simplified, two-category instantiation of the qualitative chance strip introduced in the parent module (VIR 3 precursor). The binary structure reduces cognitive load compared to the full ordering task (M2's chance line) while still requiring students to discriminate between uncertain events — directly targeting M281 (equally-likely default). The colour coding (green = HIGH, orange = LOW) should be consistent with the parent module's Slide 9 reveal (high chance in green, low chance in orange).

IAS Patterns (4)
Constrained Drag — Card-to-Zone Sort
Cognitive actions: Students pick up an event card and drag it to either the HIGH Chance or LOW Chance drop zone. Cards snap into the target zone on release; invalid drops (outside both zones) return the card to the stack. The dragging_vs_resting visual state (IAS id 49) governs the drag affordance: card enlarges to 1.1x, gains shadow, and the valid drop zones pulse with a highlighted border when the card is in flight. This is the primary interaction mechanic for the entire applet.
When to use: For every event card in the sort set — this is the core interaction loop. Used throughout the applet from first card to last.
Repair-Guided Feedback — Frequency Prompt
Cognitive actions: When a card is placed in the wrong zone, the system does not simply mark it incorrect. Instead, it displays a targeted prompt tied to the specific event: 'Think about how often this usually happens — does it happen most of the time, or almost never?' The card is returned to the stack (or held in the zone with an orange incorrect indicator) and the student must re-place it. This directly addresses M280 (vague linguistic labelling) and M283 (inability to reason between extremes) by anchoring the correction to frequency reasoning rather than abstract correctness.
When to use: On every incorrect placement. The prompt text is event-specific (pre-authored for each card in the set) to maximise relevance. Do not use generic 'Try again' feedback.
Contrastive Feedback — Pair Reveal
Cognitive actions: After the student has sorted all cards, the applet reveals a side-by-side comparison of the two most contrasting card pair (e.g., 'Rain in Bogor in January' vs 'Helicopter landing at school tomorrow') with their correct zones highlighted and a brief explanation: 'Both are uncertain — but rain in Bogor in January happens very often, while a helicopter landing at school almost never does. That is why their chance is different.' This directly targets M281 (equally-likely default) at the consolidation stage.
When to use: At the end of the sort, after all cards have been placed and the student has received individual card feedback. This is the applet's closing consolidation moment before the parent module's Exit_Question.
Highlight / Pulse / Emphasis — Drop Zone Activation
Cognitive actions: When a card is being dragged, both drop zones pulse with a coloured border (green pulse for HIGH Chance zone, orange pulse for LOW Chance zone) to signal valid drop targets. This reduces the cognitive overhead of remembering where to drop and keeps attention on the reasoning task (which zone?) rather than the motor task (where is the zone?).
When to use: During every drag operation — triggered by card pickup, deactivated on card release.
Convention Flags & False Friends (4)
These language and convention items require explicit attention in the applet design. False friends and notation conventions directly affect student comprehension in the target curriculum and geography.
chance vs probability — applet feedback strings
Risk: Applet feedback strings are the most likely place for 'probability' to leak into student-facing content. Auto-generated or templated feedback may default to 'the probability of this event is high' rather than 'this event has a high chance of happening'. This would undermine the module's deliberate deferral of the formal term to M8.
Mitigation: All applet feedback strings must use 'chance' exclusively. Review every feedback string before publication. Acceptable: 'This event has a HIGH chance of happening.' Not acceptable: 'The probability of this event is high.' This applies to correct feedback, incorrect feedback, and the closing contrastive reveal.
HIGH / LOW as binary labels — risk of false precision
Risk: Students may leave the applet believing that all events are either HIGH or LOW with no middle ground, contradicting the chance strip's continuous spectrum established in the parent module. The binary sort is a scaffold, not the final model — but if the applet does not acknowledge the simplification, it may create a new misconception.
Mitigation: Include a brief framing note at the start of the applet (or as a closing summary): 'We are sorting into HIGH and LOW to practise comparing chance — but remember, chance is a spectrum from impossible to certain. HIGH and LOW are not the only options!' This bridges back to the parent module's Slide 12 chance strip and forward to M2's chance line.
Indonesian event card contexts
Risk: If event cards use generic Western contexts (e.g., 'It will snow tomorrow', 'You will win the lottery'), they will be culturally inauthentic and may confuse Indonesian students who have no experiential reference for snow probability.
Mitigation: All event cards must use Indonesian everyday contexts. Recommended card set: (1) 'It will rain in Bogor tomorrow in January' — HIGH; (2) 'A helicopter will land at your school tomorrow' — LOW; (3) 'You will eat rice for lunch today' — HIGH; (4) 'You will score 100% on your next maths test' — LOW; (5) 'At least one student in your class will be absent this week' — HIGH; (6) 'You will find a 50,000 rupiah note on the ground today' — LOW; (7) 'The sun will rise tomorrow morning' — note: this is CERTAIN, not HIGH — use with care or replace; (8) 'Your school will be visited by the President tomorrow' — LOW. Avoid snow, lottery tickets, and other non-Indonesian contexts.
Colour coding consistency with parent module
Risk: If the applet uses different colours for HIGH/LOW than the parent module used for high/low chance labels (Slide 9: green = high, orange = low), students will experience visual discontinuity that increases cognitive load.
Mitigation: Use green for the HIGH Chance drop zone and orange/amber for the LOW Chance drop zone, matching the colour coding established in the parent module's Slide 9 reveal. Do not use red for LOW Chance — red carries a punitive connotation (IAS id 42 convention: incorrect state uses orange/yellow, not red).

Synthesis Narrative
This applet is the primary interactive vehicle for M281 — the equally-likely default misconception — and its design logic flows directly from the productive conflict established in the parent module's Slides 8–9 (Bogor rain vs helicopter contrast). Where the parent module showed students that two uncertain events can have different amounts of chance, this applet requires students to act on that insight: they must sort a set of uncertain event cards into HIGH Chance and LOW Chance zones by dragging, making the discrimination active rather than passive. The binary sort is a deliberate scaffold — it reduces the ordering task from a continuous spectrum (which belongs to M2's chance line) to a two-category decision, keeping cognitive load appropriate for the concrete CPA phase while still forcing students to reason about relative likelihood rather than treating all uncertain events as equivalent. The three misconceptions converge on a single interaction design principle: feedback must be frequency-anchored, not correctness-labelled. M280 (vague linguistic labelling) is addressed by Repair-Guided Feedback that asks 'how often does this usually happen?' rather than simply marking the placement wrong. M281 (equally-likely default) is addressed by the Contrastive Feedback pair reveal at the end of the sort, which places the two most contrasting cards side by side and explicitly names the difference in frequency. M283 (inability to reason between extremes) is addressed by including 2–3 borderline cards in the set — events that are genuinely HIGH or LOW but require reasoning rather than extreme-case recognition. The visual state requirements (IAS ids 41, 42, 49) govern the drag affordance and feedback colours: green for HIGH Chance, orange for LOW Chance, consistent with the parent module's Slide 9 colour coding. The applet's scope discipline is tight: it does not introduce the chance line (M2), does not use numerical probability (M8), and does not introduce the terms 'more likely', 'less likely', or 'equally likely' (M2 vocabulary). Its sole job is to consolidate the binary discrimination between HIGH and LOW chance using Indonesian everyday contexts, surface M281 through the contrastive pair reveal, and prepare students for the Exit_Question in the parent module's final slide. The parent module should ensure the Exit_Question (Pak Budi's two bags) follows this applet directly — the applet's closing contrastive reveal is the conceptual bridge to that diagnostic question. No Math_Trap trope belongs inside this applet; if a Math_Trap is warranted for M281 at the qualitative level, it should be placed as a module-level slide before this applet, not within the applet's interaction flow.
