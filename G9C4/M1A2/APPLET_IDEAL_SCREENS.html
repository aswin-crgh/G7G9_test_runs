Applet Blueprint
G9C4M1A2 — Learn
Sort uncertain events into 'HIGH Chance' or 'LOW Chance' categories by dragging event cards, demonstrating that chance varies across situations.
Standard Constrained Drag — Card-to-Zone Sort Target: M281 — Students believe all uncertain events are equally likely (equally-likely default), manifesting as placing all uncertain event cards in the same zone or claiming the sort is arbitrary.
Applet Screen Stages (6 stages, 6-stage IAS progression)
These are stage-level plans, not 1:1 PPTX frames. Each entry below describes ONE stage of the IAS progression (Launch → Explore → Notice → Explain → Apply → Consolidate). 'Uncertain outcome' is reinforced in the sub-caption: 'All of these events are uncertain — we cannot be sure what will happen. But do they all have the same chance?'

Misconception #280 (medium)
Students treat chance as a vague linguistic label ('probably', 'maybe', 'unlikely') and resist comparing uncertain events with any precision. In this applet's binary-sort context, the risk is that students drag cards arbitrarily — treating HIGH and LOW as interchangeable labels — rather than reasoning about which situations genuinely have more chance. The applet surfaces this when students place events like 'rain in Bogor in January' in LOW Chance or 'helicopter landing at school tomorrow' in HIGH Chance without any reasoning anchor.

Apply

The anchor pair remains visible as a reference model. Cards 3 and 5 are borderline HIGH-chance events that require reasoning beyond extreme-case recognition, directly targeting M283
Misconception #283 (medium)
Students can identify impossible and certain events but treat all uncertain events as vague and undifferentiated — they cannot reason about relative likelihood between uncertain events. In the binary sort, this surfaces as random placement of uncertain events, or as students correctly placing the extreme cases (if any appear) but failing on the middle-range events. The applet's binary sort is a simplified version of the full ordering task — it reduces the cognitive demand to a two-category decision, which is the appropriate scaffold at this concrete phase.

Scaffolding Arc
Full scaffolding at Launch (context and framing provided, no student decision required) → Partial at Explore (first 2 cards sorted with zone-pulse affordance and immediate frequency-prompt feedback on error) → Guided at Notice (contrastive pair highlighted mid-sort to surface M281 directly) → Guided-to-Independent at Explain (student articulates why two contrasting cards differ in chance) → Independent at Apply (remaining borderline cards sorted without hints, only post-placement feedback) → Independent at Consolidate (contrastive pair reveal summary, no further scaffolding).

Convention Requirements & False Friends (5)
These language conventions and false friends must be addressed at the specified stage. In the target curriculum and geography, terminology mismatches directly affect student comprehension.
chance vs probability — applet feedback strings
All student-facing text in the applet — zone labels, card prompts, correct feedback, incorrect feedback, frequency prompts, and the closing contrastive reveal — must use 'chance' exclusively. The word 'probability' must not appear anywhere in the applet. Acceptable: 'This event has a HIGH chance of happening.' Not acceptable: 'The probability of this event is high.' This applies to every screen in the applet. Review all auto-generated or templated feedback strings before publication.
Stage 2
HIGH / LOW as binary labels — risk of false precision
The applet must include an explicit framing note at Launch (Stage 1) and a closing note at Consolidate (Stage 6) acknowledging that HIGH and LOW are a scaffold, not the complete model. Required text (or equivalent): 'We are sorting into HIGH and LOW to practise comparing chance — but remember, chance is a spectrum from impossible to certain. HIGH and LOW are not the only options!' This bridges back to the parent module's chance strip and forward to M2's chance line, preventing students from leaving with a binary misconception.
Stage 1
Indonesian event card contexts
All six event cards must use authentic Indonesian everyday contexts. Required card set: (1) Rain in Bogor in January — HIGH; (2) Helicopter landing at school tomorrow — LOW; (3) Eating rice for lunch today — HIGH; (4) Scoring 100% on the next maths test — LOW; (5) At least one classmate absent this week — HIGH; (6) Finding a 50,000 rupiah note on the ground today — LOW. Do not use snow, lottery tickets, or other non-Indonesian contexts. The sun rising tomorrow is CERTAIN (not HIGH) — do not include it as a sort card.
Stage 2
Colour coding consistency with parent module
The HIGH Chance drop zone must use green; the LOW Chance drop zone must use amber/orange. Do not use red for the LOW Chance zone — red carries a punitive connotation inconsistent with IAS visual-state conventions. This colour coding must match the parent module's Slide 9 reveal (green = high chance, orange = low chance) to avoid visual discontinuity. The incorrect-placement feedback border flash must also use orange, not red.
Stage 2
false-friend: probability vs chance — feedback string audit
Before publication, conduct a full string audit across all six stages. Flag any instance of 'probability', 'probable', or 'likelihood' in student-facing text and replace with 'chance'. This is the primary false-friend risk (KB false_friend id 155, high severity) for this applet. The audit must cover: zone labels, card text, correct feedback strings, incorrect/repair feedback strings, sentence-completion options (Stage 4), and the closing contrastive reveal (Stage 6).
