Context G9C4 · Grade 9 · Probability · Kurikulum Merdeka · ID · Band 7-9
Learning Objective
Given a probability experiment, students can: (1) determine the sample space using listing, tables, or tree diagrams; (2) calculate theoretical probability P(A) = n(A)/n(S) and apply the complement rule P(Aᶜ) = 1 − P(A); (3) calculate relative frequency (empirical probability) from experimental data and explain how it converges to theoretical probability as trials increase; (4) calculate expected frequency using P(A) × k; and (5) distinguish population from sample, identify representative samples, and evaluate random sampling methods — demonstrating mastery of both theoretical and empirical probability within the Kurikulum Merdeka Grade 9 scope.
Misconception Inventory (10)
#1 — Students treat probability as a vague linguistic label ('probably', 'unlikely') rather than a precise number between 0 and 1. They resist assigning exact values and conflate everyday usage with mathematical definition.
Surfacing strategy
Present a scenario (e.g., drawing a red marble from a bag) and ask students to describe the chance in words, then in a number. Reveal the gap between 'probably not' and P = 1/5 = 0.2. Use a Math_Trap slide that shows a student saying 'it's unlikely' and asks: what is the actual probability?
Severity: highMath_TrapParameter Manipulation (probability number line placement)VIR 3
#2 — Students believe that if an event hasn't happened for a while, it is 'due' — the gambler's fallacy. They think past outcomes influence future independent trials (e.g., 'we've had 5 tails in a row, so heads is more likely next').
Surfacing strategy
Run a coin-flip simulation showing a streak of tails, then ask students to predict the next flip. Surface the gambler's fallacy explicitly via a Math_Trap slide before the simulation applet.
Severity: highMath_TrapParameter Manipulation (trial count slider)VIR 211
#3 — Students confuse relative frequency (empirical) with theoretical probability, believing that if they flip a coin 10 times and get 6 heads, then P(heads) = 6/10 = 0.6 — permanently, not just as an estimate.
Surfacing strategy
Use a convergence simulation (VIR 211): show relative frequency fluctuating wildly at low trial counts and stabilising near 0.5 as trials increase. Ask: 'Does the relative frequency equal the theoretical probability? When does it get close?'
Severity: highParameter Manipulation (trial count slider showing convergence)VIR 211
#4 — Students omit outcomes from the sample space, especially in compound events — they list some combinations but miss others (e.g., for two coins they list HH, HT, TT but forget TH, treating HT and TH as the same).
Surfacing strategy
Use a systematic table (VIR 24) to make all cells visible. Ask students to first list outcomes freely, then complete the table — the empty cells reveal omissions. A Math_Trap slide can show the incomplete list and ask 'what's missing?'
Severity: highMath_TrapDrag-to-classify (sorting outcomes into a sample space table)VIR 24
#5 — Students believe that equally likely outcomes are always the case — they assume P(head) = P(tail) = 1/2 for any two-outcome experiment, including biased coins or spinners with unequal sectors.
Surfacing strategy
Present a spinner with unequal sectors and ask for P(red). Surface the assumption via a Math_Trap: 'There are 3 colours, so P(red) = 1/3?' Correct by counting sector area/proportion.
Severity: mediumMath_TrapParameter Manipulation (spinner sector size adjustment)VIR 254
#6 — Students apply the complement rule incorrectly — they subtract from the wrong base (e.g., P(Aᶜ) = 1 − n(A) instead of 1 − P(A)), or they do not recognise when to use the complement as a shortcut.
Surfacing strategy
Present a problem where direct calculation is tedious (e.g., P(at least one head in 3 flips)) and ask students to calculate directly. Then reveal the complement shortcut. A Math_Trap can show the error of subtracting counts rather than probabilities.
Severity: mediumMath_TrapDrag-to-classify (matching events to their complements on a probability number line)VIR 3
#7 — Students confuse population and sample — they treat any group of data as a population, or they believe a large sample IS the population. They do not understand that a sample is a subset selected to represent the whole.
Surfacing strategy
Present a scenario: 'A school has 800 students. We survey 40 students about their favourite sport.' Ask: what is the population? What is the sample? Use a Math_Trap showing a student claiming '40 students IS the data' without reference to the 800.
Severity: mediumMath_TrapDrag-to-classify (sorting groups into population vs sample)
#8 — Students believe that a larger sample is always representative, regardless of how it was selected — they do not understand that a biased sampling method produces biased results even with a large n.
Surfacing strategy
Present a scenario where 500 students are surveyed but only from one school in a wealthy district. Ask: is this representative of all Indonesian students? Surface the bias via discussion before formalising random sampling methods.
Severity: mediumMath_TrapDrag-to-classify (evaluating sampling scenarios as representative or biased)
#9 — Students confuse expected frequency with guaranteed frequency — they believe P(A) × k gives the exact number of times A will occur in k trials, not the expected (average) number.
Surfacing strategy
Run a simulation of k = 60 dice rolls. Ask: how many 6s do you expect? (Expected = 10.) Then show the actual result — it will rarely be exactly 10. Discuss why 'expected' means average over many repetitions.
Severity: mediumMath_TrapParameter Manipulation (simulation showing actual vs expected frequency)VIR 211
#10 — Students treat tree diagrams as only applicable to sequential events and do not recognise that a two-way table and a tree diagram represent the same sample space — they see them as different tools for different problems rather than equivalent representations.
Surfacing strategy
Present the same compound event (e.g., flipping a coin and rolling a die) using both a tree diagram and a two-way table. Ask students to match outcomes across both representations.
Severity: lowDrag-to-classify (matching tree diagram branches to table cells)VIR 24
Visual Model Recommendations (4)
VIR 3 — Probability Number Line (0 to 1)
Rationale
Establishing probability as a measurable quantity between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain); placing events on the line; showing complement symmetry around 0.5. Entry point for the chapter — bridges everyday language to numerical probability.
Concrete introduce_concept moderate
VIR 24 — Systematic Sample Space Table (Two-Way Grid)
Rationale
Listing all outcomes of compound events systematically; counting favourable vs total cells for P(A) = n(A)/n(S); preventing omission of outcomes. Primary tool for Section A sample space work.
Pictorial introduce_concept strong
VIR 211 — Probability Simulation: Convergence of Relative Frequency
Rationale
Demonstrating that relative frequency fluctuates at low trial counts and converges toward theoretical probability as trials increase (Law of Large Numbers). Core visual for Section B empirical probability and expected frequency.
Pictorial bridge_cpa moderate
VIR 254 — Probability = Relative Frequency = Area = Proportion
Rationale
Unifying the four representations of probability (fraction, relative frequency, spinner area, proportion) into a single structural insight. Consolidation tool after students have worked with both theoretical and empirical probability.
Pictorial consolidate moderate
IAS Patterns (5)
Parameter Manipulation — Probability Number Line Placement
Cognitive actions: Students drag event markers onto a 0-to-1 number line, adjusting position to reflect estimated or calculated probability. Immediate feedback shows whether the placement is in the correct region (impossible / unlikely / equally likely / likely / certain).
When to use: Introducing probability as a quantity; surfacing the false-friend misconception between everyday language and mathematical probability (Misconception 1); complement rule visualisation (Misconception 6).
Parameter Manipulation — Trial Count Slider (Simulation)
Cognitive actions: Students adjust a slider to increase the number of trials in a coin/die/spinner simulation. The running relative frequency graph updates in real time, showing convergence toward the theoretical value. Students record relative frequency at n=10, 50, 100, 500, 1000.
When to use: Empirical vs theoretical probability (Misconception 3); gambler's fallacy (Misconception 2); expected frequency vs guaranteed frequency (Misconception 9); Law of Large Numbers convergence.
Drag-to-Classify — Sample Space Builder
Cognitive actions: Students drag outcome tiles into a two-way table or tree diagram scaffold. The applet highlights empty cells and duplicate entries, forcing systematic enumeration. Students then count favourable outcomes to compute P(A).
When to use: Systematic sample space construction (Misconception 4); equivalence of table and tree diagram representations (Misconception 10); P(A) = n(A)/n(S) calculation.
Drag-to-Classify — Population vs Sample Sorter
Cognitive actions: Students are presented with scenario cards describing groups of people or data. They drag each card into 'Population' or 'Sample' bins, then evaluate whether the sample is representative or biased. Feedback explains why each classification is correct.
When to use: Population vs sample distinction (Misconception 7); representative vs biased sampling (Misconception 8); evaluating random sampling methods.
Parameter Manipulation — Spinner Sector Adjuster
Cognitive actions: Students resize spinner sectors by dragging boundaries. The applet recalculates P(each colour) in real time. Students observe that unequal sectors produce unequal probabilities, breaking the equally-likely assumption.
When to use: Equally-likely outcomes misconception (Misconception 5); probability as proportion of area (VIR 254).
Convention Flags (7)
Probability notation P(A)
Risk: Kurikulum Merdeka textbooks use P(A) consistently, but some Indonesian classroom traditions write P(kejadian A) or use the letter K for kejadian. Students may encounter inconsistent notation across teachers and resources.
Mitigation: Establish P(A) as the standard notation throughout all modules. Define A as the event label and make clear that the letter inside the parentheses names the event, not the outcome.
Sample space notation S vs Ω
Risk: International textbooks use Ω (omega) for sample space; Kurikulum Merdeka uses S. Students who consult external resources may encounter Ω and be confused.
Mitigation: Use S consistently for sample space throughout. In a Math_Vault or convention note, acknowledge that some books use Ω and that both mean the same thing.
Complement notation Aᶜ vs A' vs Ā
Risk: Three notations are in common use: Aᶜ (superscript c), A' (prime), and Ā (A-bar). Kurikulum Merdeka Grade 9 textbooks use Aᶜ. Students may see A' in older resources.
Mitigation: Use Aᶜ as the primary notation. Acknowledge A' as an equivalent alternative in a convention note. Do not use Ā as it is less standard at this level.
Relative frequency formula presentation
Risk: Some resources write relative frequency as f/n (frequency over total trials) while others write it as the ratio of occurrences to total. The variable names differ across textbooks.
Mitigation: Define relative frequency explicitly as (number of times A occurred) ÷ (total number of trials), and introduce the formula with consistent variable names aligned to the Kurikulum Merdeka textbook.
Expected frequency formula P(A) × k
Risk: No KB lo_code exists for expected frequency. The variable k (number of trials) may conflict with k used for other purposes in algebra modules. Some resources use n instead of k.
Mitigation: Use k consistently for the number of trials in the expected frequency formula P(A) × k, and distinguish it clearly from n(A) (number of favourable outcomes) and n(S) (size of sample space).
Tree diagram orientation (left-to-right vs top-to-bottom)
Risk: Tree diagrams are drawn left-to-right in some textbooks and top-to-bottom in others. Inconsistency can confuse students when reading branching paths.
Mitigation: Adopt left-to-right orientation consistently across all modules, matching the Kurikulum Merdeka textbook convention.
Probability as fraction vs decimal vs percentage
Risk: Students may express probability as a percentage (e.g., 50%) rather than a fraction or decimal, which is technically correct but can cause errors when applying formulas like P(A) × k.
Mitigation: Accept all three forms (fraction, decimal, percentage) as correct representations of probability, but require students to convert to fraction or decimal before applying P(A) × k or the complement rule.

Synthesis Narrative
This chapter spans five conceptually distinct but interconnected probability strands, and the central instructional challenge is building a coherent progression from informal likelihood language to formal theoretical probability, then outward to empirical probability, expected frequency, and finally to the statistical infrastructure of sampling. The entry point — VIR 3, the probability number line — is not merely a warm-up; it is the conceptual anchor that resolves the chapter's most pervasive false friend (Misconception 1): students arrive with a rich everyday vocabulary of 'probably', 'unlikely', and 'certain' that feels sufficient but is mathematically imprecise. Placing events on a 0-to-1 number line forces the transition from register to quantity, and the complement rule (P(Aᶜ) = 1 − P(A)) emerges naturally as a symmetry around 0.5 on that same line. The Math_Trap trope is the right vehicle for surfacing Misconceptions 1 and 6 early, before students have calcified their informal intuitions into procedural errors. The sample space cluster (Misconceptions 4 and 10) is best resolved through the systematic two-way table (VIR 24, strong evidence), which makes omitted outcomes visible as empty cells — a concrete, self-correcting structure. The Drag-to-Classify Sample Space Builder archetype operationalises this: students who list outcomes freely will miss TH when they have HT, but the table's grid forces them to populate every cell. The tree diagram gap (no KB VIR at Grade 9 scope) means the blueprint builder must design a tree diagram visual from scratch, but it should be introduced as a parallel representation to the table, not a replacement — Misconception 10 (treating them as different tools) is resolved precisely by showing both representations side by side for the same experiment. The convergence from concrete (physical coin flips) to pictorial (table/tree) to abstract (P(A) = n(A)/n(S)) follows the CPA spine naturally across the Section A modules. The empirical probability cluster (Misconceptions 2, 3, and 9) is where the simulation archetype (Parameter Manipulation — Trial Count Slider, VIR 211) does its heaviest lifting. The gambler's fallacy and the conflation of relative frequency with theoretical probability are both rooted in the same cognitive error: treating short-run results as definitive. The simulation makes the Law of Large Numbers visible — students watch relative frequency oscillate wildly at n=10 and stabilise near the theoretical value at n=1000 — and this single interactive experience addresses all three misconceptions in sequence. Expected frequency (P(A) × k) then becomes the forward application of this insight: if relative frequency converges to P(A), then in k trials we expect P(A) × k occurrences on average, not exactly. The Math_Trap for Misconception 9 should follow the simulation, not precede it, so students have the experiential grounding to understand why 'expected' is an average. The sampling methodology cluster (Section D, Misconceptions 7 and 8) is the chapter's largest KB gap — no visual models, no misconception IDs, no IAS archetypes exist in the KB for this content. The Drag-to-Classify Population vs Sample Sorter is a gap-filled design that addresses the conceptual distinction, but the blueprint builder will need to design the random sampling simulator from scratch. The structural isomorphism between probability as relative frequency and sampling as a proportion (VIR 254) provides a conceptual bridge: a representative sample is one whose relative frequencies mirror the population's — the same convergence logic that governs the Law of Large Numbers applies here at the level of sampling design.
