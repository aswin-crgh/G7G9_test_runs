Applet Blueprint
G7C4M1A2 — Learn
Identify a changing quantity in a concrete everyday context (apples on a plate vs. apples in a sack) and distinguish it from a fixed quantity, as the first step toward representing changing quantities with a letter (variable).
Standard Progressive Reveal Target: M252 — concrete-phase precursor: students read the letter 'a' as an abbreviation for the fruit name ('apple') rather than as a label for the count of apples ('how many apples are in the sack').
Applet Screen Stages (6 stages, 6-stage IAS progression)

1
Launch
The screen opens on a warm Indonesian kitchen scene. Candra is preparing a fruit plate for guests. A narrated caption reads: 'Candra puts 3 apples on a plate for his guests. His father also brought home a sack of apples — but the sack is tied shut.' Two objects appear on screen: a plate with 3 clearly visible apples, and a tied cloth sack beside it. No interaction is required. The purpose is to establish the scenario and create a felt sense of contrast: one quantity is visible and countable, the other is hidden and unknown. A prompt at the bottom reads: 'Look carefully at the plate and the sack. What do you notice?'

2
Explore
The plate and sack from Stage 1 remain on screen. A guiding prompt appears: 'Tap the plate to count the apples.' When the student taps the plate, each apple is highlighted one by one with a count (1, 2, 3) and a label appears: 'Apples on the plate: 3 — we can count them!' Then a second prompt appears: 'Now tap the sack.' When the student taps the sack, it shakes gently but does not open. A label appears: 'Apples in the sack: ? — we cannot see inside!' A side-by-side layout then locks in: left column shows the plate with '3' beneath it; right column shows the sack with '?' beneath it. A guiding caption reads: 'One quantity we know. One quantity we do not know.' This is the perceptual foundation for the fixed/changing distinction — students experience the contrast through their own tapping actions before any classification vocabulary is introduced.

3
Notice
The side-by-side layout from Stage 2 remains. Two classification buttons appear beneath each object: 'Fixed quantity' and 'Changing quantity'. A prompt reads: 'Tap to classify each quantity before we check.' Student must first classify the plate (3 apples): if they tap 'Fixed quantity', a green tick appears and a confirmation reads 'Correct! We always know there are 3 apples on the plate — this quantity does not change.' If they tap 'Changing quantity', a gentle red highlight fires with feedback: 'Not quite — we can always count exactly 3 apples on the plate. It stays the same.' Student then classifies the sack: if they tap 'Changing quantity', a green tick appears with 'Correct! We do not know how many apples are in the sack — this quantity could be different each time.' If they tap 'Fixed quantity', feedback reads: 'Not quite — we cannot see inside the sack. The number of apples there could be anything!' After both are correctly classified, the two-column table locks in with labels: left = 'Fixed quantity: 3' and right = 'Changing quantity: ?'. This is the Commit-Before-Reveal moment that secures the perceptual distinction.

Explain
The locked two-column table from Stage 3 is visible at the top. A new prompt appears: 'We want to write something to represent the number of apples in the sack. We will use a letter.' The letter 'a' appears beside the sack image. A multiple-choice question fires: 'What does the letter a mean here? Choose one: (A) a is the name of the fruit — apple. (B) a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack.' If the student selects (A) — the distractor — Counterexample Feedback fires: 'We already know the fruit is called an apple! The letter a is not a name for the fruit. a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack — it could be 5, or 10, or 20. We do not know yet, so we use a letter.' A visual shows the sack with possible counts (5? 10? 20?) animating inside a thought bubble. If the student selects (B) — the correct answer — confirmatory feedback fires: 'Exactly! a does not name the fruit. a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack.' The table updates: right column now reads 'Changing quantity: a (how many apples in the sack)'. A callout box appears: 'a is not the name of the fruit. a = HOW MANY apples are in the sack.' Misconception #252 (medium)
At this applet's concrete scope, the M252 precursor is the tendency to treat the letter 'a' (for apples in the sack) as a name for the fruit rather than a count of the fruit — i.e., 'a means apple, not how many apples.' The module trace confirms this is explicitly addressed at Slide 8 with the callout 'The letter a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack — it is not a name for the apples.' This applet is the source of that correction. The deeper M252 form (variable as mystery unknown to solve for) is not yet activatable at this applet's scope because no algebraic notation or equation is present.

5
Apply
A Match Pairs interaction. Three quantity description cards appear in a shuffled stack: (1) '3 apples on the plate', (2) 'apples in Candra's sack (represented by a)', (3) 'oranges in Candra's father's sack'. Two target columns are displayed: 'Fixed quantity — we know the exact number' and 'Changing quantity — we use a letter'. Student drags each card to the correct column. No guiding prompts are shown. Correct placements: card 1 → Fixed; cards 2 and 3 → Changing. After all three are placed, the system checks: correct placements receive a green tick; incorrect placements are returned to the stack with brief feedback ('Try again — can we count this exactly?'). When all three are correctly placed, a summary row appears beneath the Changing column: 'We use a letter because we do not know the exact number.' This interaction extends the fixed/changing distinction to a second unknown quantity (oranges in the father's sack), preparing the Shout_Out_Loud moment in Stage 6 and directly enacting the Match Pairs archetype from the research brief.

6
Consolidate
A Shout_Out_Loud moment fires first: the full two-column table from the applet is displayed — plate (Fixed: 3) and apple sack (Changing: a). A choral-response prompt reads: 'Say it together: a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack — not the name of the fruit!' Then the Exit Question appears: 'Candra's father also brought a sack of oranges. We do not know how many oranges are inside. Which letter could we use to represent the number of oranges in the sack?' Four tap options appear: (A) o — because o stands for orange, (B) b — because we need a different letter from a, (C) 3 — because there might be 3 oranges, (D) ? — because we do not know. The correct answer is (B). If the student taps (A), feedback fires: 'Careful — we do not choose letters because they match the fruit name. We already know it is an orange! We choose any letter that is different from a, because a and b represent different quantities.' If the student taps (B), confirmatory feedback fires: 'Correct! We use b — or any letter that is not already used — to represent how many oranges are in the sack. The letter is not the name of the fruit; it is a label for HOW MANY.' A final summary panel reads: 'Fixed quantity: we know the number (e.g., 3 apples on the plate). Changing quantity: we use a letter to represent how many (e.g., a for apples in the sack, b for oranges in the sack).' A forward pointer appears: 'Next: we will give these ideas their proper names — variable and constant.'

Scaffolding Arc
Stage 1 (Launch): fully guided observation — teacher-narrated reveal of the scenario with no student decision required. Stage 2 (Explore): partial scaffolding — student taps to reveal the sack and observes the contrast between countable and uncountable quantities with guiding prompts. Stage 3 (Notice): guided — student commits to classifying each quantity as fixed or changing before confirmation, with immediate corrective feedback. Stage 4 (Explain): guided-to-independent — student selects the correct meaning of the letter 'a' from two options; Counterexample Feedback fires on the distractor; student articulates the 'HOW MANY' principle. Stage 5 (Apply): independent — student drags three quantity descriptions to Fixed or Changing columns without prompts, receiving only correctness feedback. Stage 6 (Consolidate): independent — student reads the summary and answers the Exit Question by selecting the correct letter label for a new unknown quantity (oranges in a sack), with no scaffolding.

Convention Requirements & False Friends (4)
These language conventions and false friends must be addressed at the specified stage. In the target curriculum and geography, terminology mismatches directly affect student comprehension.
Letter 'a' as quantity label vs. fruit name
The letter 'a' must be introduced ONLY after the sack scenario is established and the question 'how many apples are in the sack?' has been posed. Student-facing text must frame 'a' as answering 'how many?' — never as a name or abbreviation. The canonical callout 'a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack — it is not the name of the fruit' must appear verbatim at Stage 4 (Explain) and be echoed in the Stage 6 (Consolidate) feedback for distractor (A). This is the concrete-phase surface of M252.
Stage 4
Fixed quantity vs. constant — vocabulary sequencing
This applet operates at the concrete phase and must use only the everyday labels 'fixed quantity' and 'changing quantity' in all student-facing text. The formal terms 'constant' and 'variable' must NOT appear in any student-facing label, button, or feedback string within this applet. A forward pointer to 'variable' and 'constant' is permitted only in the Stage 6 (Consolidate) summary panel as a bridge to the module's Math_Vault slide.
Stage 6
Letter choice: 'a' for apples, 'b' for oranges — arbitrary letter principle
When 'b' is introduced for oranges in Stage 5 (Apply) and Stage 6 (Consolidate), student-facing text must explicitly state that any letter (not just 'b') could be used — the choice is arbitrary — and that a different letter is needed because 'a' and 'b' represent different quantities. The Exit Question distractor (A) — 'o because o stands for orange' — must trigger Counterexample Feedback that directly addresses the abbreviation misconception: 'We do not choose letters because they match the fruit name.'
Stage 6
Shout_Out_Loud sequencing — fires after classification is secure
The Shout_Out_Loud choral-response prompt ('Say it together: a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack — not the name of the fruit!') must appear at Stage 6 (Consolidate), AFTER the fixed/changing classification interaction in Stage 5 (Apply) is complete. It must not fire before the student has successfully classified all three quantity cards, so the letter introduction feels like a natural extension of the established pattern rather than an arbitrary label.
