Misconception Inventory (3)
#252 — At this applet's concrete scope, the M252 precursor is the tendency to treat the letter 'a' (for apples in the sack) as a name for the fruit rather than a count of the fruit — i.e., 'a means apple, not how many apples.' The module trace confirms this is explicitly addressed at Slide 8 with the callout 'The letter a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack — it is not a name for the apples.' This applet is the source of that correction. The deeper M252 form (variable as mystery unknown to solve for) is not yet activatable at this applet's scope because no algebraic notation or equation is present.
Surfacing strategy
Within the applet interaction: after the student assigns the letter 'a' to the sack of apples, present a distractor choice — 'Does a mean: (A) the apple fruit, or (B) how many apples are in the sack?' If the student selects (A), deploy Counterexample Feedback: 'a is not the name of the fruit — we already know it's an apple! a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack.' This is the concrete-phase surface of M252.

VIR 161 — Variable as Pattern Continuation
A variable isn't a mysterious 'x' to solve for — it's the rule that describes a pattern. If the pattern is 2, 4, 6, 8, ..., the variable expression is 2n. Students discover variables by generalizing patterns they already see.

Visual Model Recommendations (1)
VIR 161 — Variable as Pattern Continuation
Rationale
Primary visual anchor for this applet. Ground the distinction between a fixed quantity (3 apples on the plate — countable, visible, known) and a changing/unknown quantity (apples in the sack — not countable, not visible, unknown). The fruit-sack scenario is the concrete instantiation of VIR 161's core insight: a letter is not a mystery to solve but a label for 'how many' — a quantity that could be any number. Position at the opening moment of the applet, before any letter is introduced, so students perceive the fixed/changing distinction in the physical scenario first.

IAS Patterns (5)
Progressive Reveal
Cognitive actions: Attend, Perceive Structure
When to use: Use to introduce the two quantities sequentially: first show the plate with 3 apples (fixed, countable), then reveal the sack (unknown, not countable). The sequential reveal forces students to perceive the contrast before they are asked to classify. This is the concrete-phase entry into the fixed/changing distinction.
Side-by-Side Contrast
Cognitive actions: Compare, Map Representations, Perceive Structure
When to use: Use to display the plate (known quantity = 3) alongside the sack (unknown quantity = a) after the reveal, so students can directly compare the two types of quantity. The two-column known-vs-unknown layout from the module trace (Slide 8) is the correct realisation of this archetype at concrete phase.
Counterexample Feedback
Cognitive actions: Demonstrate a case that breaks a false rule the learner proposed
When to use: Deploy when a student selects the distractor 'a means the apple fruit' — show that the fruit already has a name ('apple') and that the letter a must therefore mean something else: how many. This is the concrete-phase surface of M252 and should fire as immediate corrective feedback within the applet interaction.
Commit-Before-Reveal
Cognitive actions: Predict, Commit
When to use: Use at the classification moment: ask the student to tap/select whether the number of apples on the plate is a 'fixed quantity' or a 'changing quantity' before the answer is confirmed. Locks in the student's reasoning and increases engagement with the distinction. Repeat for the sack quantity.
Match Pairs
Cognitive actions: Map Representations, Compare, Classify
When to use: Use as the applet's consolidation interaction: present two or three quantity descriptions (e.g., '3 apples on the plate', 'apples in the sack', 'oranges in Candra's father's sack') and ask students to drag each to the correct column (Fixed / Changing). This is the Shout_Out_Loud moment in the applet and directly prepares students for the module's variable/constant vocabulary formalisation.
Convention Flags & False Friends (4)
These language and convention items require explicit attention in the applet design. False friends and notation conventions directly affect student comprehension in the target curriculum and geography.
Letter 'a' as quantity label vs. fruit name
Risk: When students first see 'a apples', they may read 'a' as an abbreviation for 'apple' (the fruit) rather than as a label for the count of apples. This is the concrete-phase surface of M252 and is the single most important convention to establish in this applet. The module trace confirms this risk is real — the Slide 8 callout was specifically designed to address it.
Mitigation: Introduce the letter 'a' only after the sack scenario is established and the question 'how many apples are in the sack?' has been posed. Frame the letter explicitly as answering the question 'how many?' — not as a name. Use the callout: 'a tells us HOW MANY apples are in the sack — it is not the name of the fruit.'
Fixed quantity vs. constant — vocabulary sequencing
Risk: The applet operates at the concrete phase and uses the everyday language 'fixed quantity' and 'changing quantity'. The formal vocabulary 'constant' and 'variable' is introduced in the module's Math_Vault (Slide 9 in the module trace). If the applet introduces 'constant' and 'variable' prematurely, it front-loads abstract vocabulary before the concrete distinction is secure.
Mitigation: Use only 'fixed quantity' and 'changing quantity' (or 'known quantity' and 'unknown quantity') as the student-facing labels in this applet. Reserve 'constant' and 'variable' for the module's Math_Vault slide that follows this applet in the sequence. The applet's summary slide (Slide 31 in the topic) uses 'b means THE NUMBER of oranges' — this phrasing is correct and should be preserved.
Letter choice: 'a' for apples, 'b' for oranges
Risk: Using 'a' for apples and 'b' for oranges risks reinforcing the misconception that variable letters are abbreviations for the object's name (a = apple, b = banana/orange). The module trace confirms this risk and addresses it with the 'HOW MANY' callout. The applet's summary (Slide 31) correctly states 'Any letter except a can be used for number of oranges' — this is the right message but must be delivered carefully.
Mitigation: After establishing 'a' for the apple sack, explicitly demonstrate that any letter (not just 'a') could have been used — the choice of letter is arbitrary. When introducing 'b' for oranges, state: 'We use b here, but we could use any letter — as long as it is different from a, because a and b represent different quantities.' This directly addresses the abbreviation misconception.
Shout_Out_Loud interaction — oranges in the sack
Risk: The applet summary (Slide 31) shows a Shout_Out_Loud template asking 'How many oranges are in the sack Candra's father brought?' with the answer 'We don't know — that's why we use a letter!' This is the correct concrete framing. The risk is that the Shout_Out_Loud fires before the fixed/changing distinction is secure, making the letter introduction feel arbitrary.
Mitigation: Sequence the Shout_Out_Loud after the fixed/changing classification interaction is complete. The question 'How many oranges?' should feel like a natural extension of the established pattern: 'We couldn't count the apples in the sack, and we can't count the oranges either — so we use a different letter, b, to represent how many oranges there are.'

Synthesis Narrative
This applet is the module's concrete-phase entry point — the moment when students first encounter the idea that some quantities can be counted (fixed) and some cannot (changing/unknown), and that a letter can stand in for the count of the latter. Its instructional job is narrow and precise: establish the perceptual distinction between a fixed quantity and a changing quantity using a familiar Indonesian everyday context (Candra's fruit scenario), and introduce the letter 'a' as a label for 'how many apples are in the sack' — not as a name for the fruit. Everything that follows in the module (the Math_Vault formalisation of 'variable' and 'constant', the Math_Trap for M252, the notation 3n) depends on this perceptual foundation being secure. The recommended interaction sequence flows directly from the KB evidence. VIR 161 (Variable as Pattern Continuation) is the primary visual anchor, adapted here to the static fruit-sack scenario rather than a growing pattern: the plate of 3 apples is the 'fixed' element (like the constant in a pattern), and the sack of unknown apples is the 'changing' element (like the variable). The Progressive Reveal archetype establishes the contrast sequentially — plate first, then sack — before any classification is demanded. The Side-by-Side Contrast archetype then holds both quantities in view simultaneously, enabling the Commit-Before-Reveal classification interaction: students tap to identify which quantity is fixed and which is changing before the answer is confirmed. This sequence mirrors the Exploration 4.1 table structure from the textbook (p.127) at the most concrete level, before any table or notation is introduced. The applet's single most important convention risk is the letter-as-abbreviation confusion: students seeing 'a apples' may read 'a' as shorthand for 'apple' rather than as a count. The Counterexample Feedback archetype is the correct response mechanism — 'We already know it's an apple; a tells us HOW MANY.' The Shout_Out_Loud moment (oranges in Candra's father's sack) extends the pattern to a second unknown quantity and introduces 'b', reinforcing that letter choice is arbitrary and that different unknowns need different letters. This is the applet's consolidation moment and directly prepares students for the module's Math_Vault formalisation of 'variable'. M251 and M250 are not activatable at this applet's scope and require no instructional action here; the parent module's Math_Trap slides are the correct venue for those misconceptions once algebraic notation is introduced.
