Learning Objective

Misconception Inventory (3)
#252 — Students believe variables are mysterious unknown numbers to be 'solved for', rather than placeholders that represent any value in a pattern or rule. In this module's context, students see 'n' and immediately try to find its value rather than accepting it as a label for 'any pattern number'. This is the primary target misconception for this module.
Surfacing strategy
Present a completed table (pattern 1→3, 2→6, 3→9) and the expression 3n, then ask: 'What is n?' If the student says 'I need to solve for n' or writes a single number, the misconception is active. The Math_Trap slide should show a student 'solving' 3n = 12 and ask whether this is the right move.
Severity: mediumMath_TrapCounterexample FeedbackVIR 161 — Variable as Pattern Continuation
VIR 161 — Variable as Pattern Continuation
Primary visual anchor for this module. Ground the variable 'n' as the generalisation of a pattern students already see — Nyoman's matchstick squares grow by 3 each time, so the changing quantity is 3 × (pattern number) = 3n. This directly counters M252 (variable as mystery unknown) by showing that n is not a hidden number to find but a label for 'whichever pattern number we are at'. Position at the Snapshot moment immediately after the opening context is established.
CPAconcrete
#251 — Although the full expression-evaluation error (treating 2 + 3x as 5x) belongs to sibling module G7C4M2, a precursor form is relevant here: students may see '1 + 3n' (from Exploration 4.1) and attempt to combine the constant 1 with the variable term 3n into '4n', treating unlike parts as combinable. This is the closure/premature-simplification precursor at the concrete phase.
Surfacing strategy
After deriving 1 + 3n from Nyoman's matchstick pattern, ask: 'Can we simplify 1 + 3n further?' If the student writes 4n, the precursor misconception is active. Use a Counterexample Feedback response: substitute n = 2 to show 1 + 3(2) = 7, not 4(2) = 8.

IAS Patterns (5)
Progressive Reveal
Cognitive actions: Attend, Perceive Structure
When to use: Use to build the matchstick pattern one step at a time — show figure 1, then figure 2, then figure 3 — so students observe the growing quantity before the rule is named. This is the concrete-phase entry into the LO and sets up the Snapshot moment.
Representation Translation
Cognitive actions: Compare, Map Representations, Transfer
When to use: Use to connect the three representations of the same pattern: (1) the visual matchstick figure, (2) the table of fixed vs. changing quantities, and (3) the algebraic form 3n. Students move between all three, confirming that 3n is just a compact way of writing what the table already shows. This is the core CPA bridge for this module.
Counterexample Feedback
Cognitive actions: Demonstrate a case that breaks a false rule the learner proposed
When to use: Deploy in the Math_Trap slide for M252: after a student 'solves for n', show that substituting n = 1, 2, 3 all give valid answers — there is no single correct value of n. Also use for M251 precursor: substitute n = 2 into both 1 + 3n and the incorrect 4n to show they give different answers.
Commit-Before-Reveal
Cognitive actions: Predict, Commit
When to use: Use in the Exit_Question and in the fixed-vs-changing table activity: ask students to predict whether a quantity is fixed or changing before the pattern is extended. Locks in the student's reasoning before the answer is shown, increasing engagement with the variable/constant distinction.
Rule Completion
Cognitive actions: Generalize, Reflect
When to use: Use after the pattern table is complete: present '3 × n = ___' and ask students to write the algebraic form. Then extend to the full expression 1 + 3n. This is the generalisation step that moves from the table to the notation, and is the moment where the Math_Vault definition of 'algebraic form' is anchored.
Convention Flags (4)
Implicit multiplication in 3n
Risk: Students from arithmetic backgrounds expect an explicit multiplication sign (3 × n) and may not recognise 3n as '3 times n'. This is the single most critical notation shift in this module and is introduced here for the first time in the chapter.
Mitigation: Show 3 × n = 3n side by side in the Math_Vault slide. Use colour-coding: the coefficient 3 in one colour, the variable n in another. Explicitly state: 'In algebra, we drop the × sign when a number multiplies a letter.' Reinforce in every worked example in this module.
Variable n as 'any value' vs. 'the unknown value'
Risk: The word 'unknown' appears in the topic's LO slide and summary (from the existing topic content), framing the variable as something 'not known yet' — which primes the M252 misconception. This module's LO frames n as a pattern-position label, not an unknown to solve for. The two framings conflict.
Mitigation: Explicitly distinguish the two uses of letters in algebra at the Snapshot moment: 'In a pattern, n tells us which figure we are looking at — it can be 1, 2, 3, or any number. We are not trying to find n; we are using n to describe the rule.' Avoid the word 'unknown' in this module's student-facing content.
Algebraic form vs. algebraic expression
Risk: The curriculum vocabulary requires introducing 'algebraic form' (bentuk aljabar) as the module's primary term, but the chapter-level vocabulary also includes 'algebraic expression'. These are used interchangeably in the textbook (p.129) but students may treat them as different objects.
Mitigation: Treat 'algebraic form' as the primary term for this module (matching the textbook's Exploration 4.1 language) and note in the Math_Vault that 'algebraic expression' means the same thing. Do not introduce 'algebraic expression' as a separate concept here — that disambiguation belongs to G7C4M2.
Constant '1' in 1 + 3n — fixed quantity from the pattern
Risk: In Nyoman's matchstick pattern, the constant 1 represents the fixed corner matchstick. Students may not recognise why 1 is a constant (it does not change as n changes) vs. 3n which does change. The structural reason (fixed quantity in the physical pattern) must be made explicit.
Mitigation: Use the fixed-vs-changing table from Exploration 4.1 explicitly: label the column 'Fixed quantity (constant)' and 'Changing quantity (variable term)'. Colour-code the constant 1 and the variable term 3n differently in every representation.

Synthesis Narrative
This module is the chapter's concrete-phase entry point into algebraic thinking, and its central instructional challenge is one of framing: students must encounter the letter 'n' not as a mystery number waiting to be solved, but as a label for 'whichever figure in the pattern we are looking at.' VIR 161 (Variable as Pattern Continuation) is the recommended primary visual anchor precisely because it grounds this reframing in something students already understand — a growing sequence. Nyoman's matchstick square pattern from Exploration 4.1 (p.127) is the ideal concrete context: the pattern grows by 3 matchsticks per figure, the fixed corner matchstick is always 1, and the algebraic form 1 + 3n emerges naturally from the table of fixed vs. changing quantities. The Snapshot trope should fire at the moment the table is complete and the pattern is visible, before any algebraic notation is introduced — this is the perceptual anchor that all subsequent abstraction will reference. The path from the concrete table to the abstract notation 3n runs through two critical instructional moves. First, the fixed-vs-changing distinction must be made explicit and colour-coded: the constant 1 is always the same regardless of which figure we are at, while 3n changes because it depends on n. This distinction is the conceptual foundation for the vocabulary terms 'variable' and 'constant', which the Math_Vault trope should formalise immediately after the table activity. Second, the implicit multiplication convention (3n means 3 × n) must be introduced explicitly — this is the single most important notation shift in the module, and the conventions flag is high-priority. The Representation Translation archetype (IAS 4) is the workhorse here: students move between the visual figure, the table, and the expression 3n, confirming at each step that all three say the same thing. VIR 203 (Function Machines) then bridges to the abstract notation by framing 3n as a rule: input the pattern number, multiply by 3, get the number of matchsticks added. This positions the expression as a description of a process, not a number to solve. The two misconceptions requiring active surfacing in this module are M252 and the precursor form of M251. M252 (variable as mystery unknown) is the higher-priority target: the Math_Trap slide should present a student who, upon seeing 3n, immediately asks 'what is n?' and begins solving. The Counterexample Feedback archetype (IAS 33) is the correct response mechanism — show that n = 1 gives 3, n = 2 gives 6, n = 3 gives 9, and ask: 'Which of these is the right answer for n?' The answer is all of them, depending on which figure we are describing. The M251 precursor (combining 1 + 3n into 4n) should be surfaced in a second Math_Trap: after the full expression 1 + 3n is derived, ask whether it can be simplified further. If a student writes 4n, the Counterexample Feedback fires: substitute n = 2 to show 1 + 3(2) = 7 ≠ 4(2) = 8. M250 (guess-and-check equation solving) is linked in the KB checklist but is not activated by this module's LO and requires no instructional action here. The Exit_Question should use the Commit-Before-Reveal archetype: present a new growing pattern (e.g., a sequence of tile arrangements) and ask students to write the algebraic form, committing before the answer is revealed.
