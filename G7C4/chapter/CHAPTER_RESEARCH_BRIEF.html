Learning Objective
Given a real-world or mathematical situation, students can: (1) identify and name variables, coefficients, constants, and terms in an algebraic expression; (2) evaluate algebraic expressions by substitution; (3) prove equivalence of algebraic forms using commutative, associative, and distributive properties; (4) add and subtract algebraic expressions by collecting like terms; and (5) translate word problems and contextual situations into algebraic expressions and use them to solve problems — demonstrating mastery of algebraic form structure, manipulation, and modeling at the abstract level.
Visual Model Recommendations (5)
VIR 213 — The Distributive Property Is Everywhere
Rationale
Introduce the distributive property as a unifying principle across arithmetic and algebraic expansion; show that expanding brackets (e.g., 3(x+2) = 3x+6) is the same structural move as breaking apart multiplication in arithmetic (7×8 = 7×5 + 7×3). Grounds LO sub-skill 3 (equivalence via distributive property) and LO sub-skill 4 (collecting like terms after expansion).
Pictorial introduce_concept strong
VIR 161 — Variable as Pattern Continuation
Rationale
Anchor the concept of a variable as a generalisation of a pattern rather than an unknown to solve for. Supports LO sub-skill 1 (identifying variables) and LO sub-skill 5 (translating contextual situations into algebraic expressions). Particularly effective as an entry point before formal vocabulary is introduced.
Concrete introduce_concept moderate
VIR 203 — Function Machines: Input → Rule → Output
Rationale
Bridge from arithmetic operations to algebraic substitution. The input→rule→output metaphor makes substitution (LO sub-skill 2) concrete: the expression is the rule, the substituted value is the input, and the result is the output. Useful before introducing formal substitution notation.
Pictorial bridge_cpa moderate
VIR 214 — Every Equation Is a Balance
Rationale
Support equivalence reasoning (LO sub-skill 3): two algebraic forms are equivalent when they always produce the same value — like two sides of a balanced scale. Useful for the substitution-as-equivalence-test approach flagged in the KB gap for Exploration 4.2.
Pictorial bridge_cpa moderate
VIR 237 — Add Zero in Disguise: The Additive Identity Trick
Rationale
Consolidate understanding of algebraic manipulation: adding and subtracting like terms is equivalent to adding zero in disguise (e.g., 3x + 5x − 5x = 3x). Supports LO sub-skill 4 (collecting like terms) and deepens understanding of why algebraic equivalence holds.
Abstract consolidate moderate
IAS Patterns (5)
Representation Translation
Cognitive actions: Students move between concrete representations (coin/tile models, area diagrams) and symbolic algebraic expressions, building the CPA bridge from pictorial to abstract.
When to use: LO sub-skills 1 and 5: naming parts of an expression and translating word problems into algebraic form. Use coin/variable tile models (IAS 61) and area model (IAS 63) to make the translation from context to symbol explicit.
Simulation & Modeling
Cognitive actions: Students manipulate a balance-scale model to test whether two algebraic forms are equivalent, observing that substituting the same value into both sides yields equal outputs.
When to use: LO sub-skill 3 (equivalence via properties) and LO sub-skill 2 (substitution). Use IAS 62 (Equality Explorer) to make the commutative, associative, and distributive properties visible as balance-preserving moves.
Guided Discovery
Cognitive actions: Students follow a scaffolded sequence — observe a pattern, generalise it with a variable expression, verify by substitution — to construct understanding of algebraic structure.
When to use: LO sub-skill 1 (identifying expression parts) and LO sub-skill 5 (modeling). Scaffolded discovery sequences (IAS 7, IAS 2) are appropriate for the Section A vocabulary introduction and Section C word-problem modeling.
Parameter Manipulation
Cognitive actions: Students change the value of a variable and observe how the expression's value changes, building intuition for substitution and the role of coefficients.
When to use: LO sub-skill 2 (substitution) and LO sub-skill 1 (coefficient as multiplier). Slider-based parameter manipulation (IAS 66, IAS 1) makes the coefficient's role as a scaling factor visceral.
Construction & Creation
Cognitive actions: Students build algebraic expressions from contextual descriptions, selecting variables, assigning coefficients, and writing the expression that models the situation.
When to use: LO sub-skill 5 (translating word problems). Students construct the expression themselves rather than matching or selecting, requiring full encoding of the contextual structure into algebraic form.
Convention Flags (9)
Coefficient notation: implicit multiplication
Risk: Students from arithmetic backgrounds expect an explicit multiplication sign (3 × x) and may not recognise 3x as '3 times x'. This is a notation shift that is not always explicitly taught.
Mitigation: Introduce implicit multiplication explicitly in the first module: show 3 × x = 3x side by side, and use colour-coding to distinguish coefficient from variable in early examples.
Exponent notation: n × n = n²
Risk: Students may write n × n as 2n (doubling confusion) rather than n². This is a KB-flagged gap with no explicit lo_code at Grade 7. The textbook introduces this in Section B but the KB has no misconception record for it.
Mitigation: Explicitly contrast 2n (two groups of n) with n² (n groups of n) using area model visuals. Flag as a KB insertion gap.
Like terms: variable part must match exactly
Risk: Students may treat 3x and 3x² as like terms because both contain x, or treat 3x and 3y as like terms because both have coefficient 3. The 'like' criterion requires identical variable-and-exponent combination.
Mitigation: Use colour-coding and sorting activities (Representation Translation archetype) to make the variable-part matching criterion visible. Explicitly contrast like and unlike term pairs.
Distributive property direction: expanding vs factoring
Risk: Students learn a(b+c) = ab+ac (expanding) but may not recognise ab+ac = a(b+c) (factoring) as the same property applied in reverse. The textbook treats both, but students often see them as unrelated procedures.
Mitigation: Present expanding and factoring as two directions of the same arrow using VIR 213 (The Distributive Property Is Everywhere). Use reversible worked examples.
Cluster mapping mismatch: gradient/functions cluster
Risk: KB cluster_mapping_id 114 maps this chapter to 'Gradient of a Straight Line' (KM.F2.KC.A) and CCSS 8.F.B (Grade 8 Functions). This is a chapter-level mismatch — this chapter covers algebraic expressions, not graphs or functions.
Mitigation: Flag cluster_mapping_id 114 as incorrect primary cluster for this chapter. The correct alignment is KM.TP.FD.ALJ.1 and KM.F1.AL.A.1–A.3. Do not use the gradient/functions cluster to scope module content.

Risk: KB evidence references CCSS 7.EE.B.4a (solving equations), which is beyond this chapter's scope. This chapter aligns to CCSS 7.EE.A.1 (apply properties of operations to add, subtract, factor, and expand linear expressions), which is absent from the KB curricula table.
Mitigation: Use CCSS 7.EE.A.1 as the cross-curriculum reference for this chapter. Flag 7.EE.B.4a as belonging to the subsequent equations chapter. Request KB insertion of 7.EE.A.1 for this chapter's lo_codes.
Graph sub-component of CR.7.AL.GR.S3.4 and CR.7.AL.GR.S4.3
Risk: These KB LOs include 'using graphs' as part of modeling. The textbook chapter does not cover graphs. Including graph-related content in this chapter's modules would be out of scope and create premature cognitive load.
Mitigation: Explicitly exclude the graph sub-component from all modules in this chapter. Assign graph-related content to the subsequent chapter on graphs/functions. Note this in module blueprints.
Term 'collect' (like terms) — polysemy
Risk: The mathematical instruction to 'collect like terms' may be interpreted through the everyday meaning of 'collect' (gather objects as a hobby, pick up), reducing precision of the instruction.
Mitigation: Bridge the everyday and mathematical meanings explicitly: frame collecting like terms as sorting and counting groups (all x-terms together, all constants together). Use the sorting metaphor consistently.
Term 'degree' (of a polynomial/term)
Risk: If 'degree' is introduced in the context of exponents (e.g., degree of a term), students may confuse it with angle degrees or academic degrees. Medium confusion risk per KB false-friend record.
Mitigation: When introducing degree of a term or expression, always qualify: 'the degree of this term means the highest power (exponent) of the variable.' Avoid using 'degree' without qualification in early instruction.

Synthesis Narrative
This chapter introduces students to the abstract language of algebra for the first time at Grade 7, making it one of the highest-stakes conceptual transitions in the Kurikulum Merdeka mathematics sequence. The three-section structure of the textbook (Section A: Elements of Algebraic Expressions; Section B: Properties and Operations; Section C: Modeling) maps cleanly onto a CPA arc that begins with naming and recognising the parts of an expression (concrete/pictorial), moves through manipulation using properties (pictorial/abstract), and culminates in translating real-world situations into algebraic models (abstract). The central instructional challenge is that students arrive with arithmetic thinking — where letters are abbreviations or unknowns to solve for — and must reconstruct their understanding of symbols as generalised quantities. VIR 161 (Variable as Pattern Continuation) is the recommended entry point: by grounding the variable in a pattern students already see (2, 4, 6, 8, … → 2n), the chapter avoids the 'x is a mystery number' framing that drives the most persistent early-algebra misconceptions. From this foundation, VIR 203 (Function Machines) bridges to substitution by making the expression a rule and the variable value an input, directly scaffolding LO sub-skill 2 before formal notation is introduced. The manipulation strand (LO sub-skills 3 and 4) is anchored by VIR 213 (The Distributive Property Is Everywhere), which has strong evidence strength and is the most pedagogically powerful visual in the KB for this chapter. The key instructional move is to present expanding and factoring as two directions of the same structural principle — not as separate procedures — using reversible worked examples and the area model interactive (IAS 63). The Representation Translation archetype (IAS 61, 63) is the workhorse for this strand: students move between area diagrams, tile models, and symbolic expressions, building the representational fluency needed to recognise equivalent forms. The Simulation & Modeling archetype (IAS 62: Equality Explorer) then provides the equivalence-testing mechanism for LO sub-skill 3: students substitute the same value into two algebraic forms and observe that the balance scale remains level, making the commutative and associative properties visible as balance-preserving moves rather than abstract rules. VIR 214 (Every Equation Is a Balance) supports this at the pictorial level. The most significant structural risk for this chapter is the complete absence of KB misconception records (the lo_misconceptions_checklist is empty). This is not a content gap — the misconceptions are well-known from research literature — but a KB data gap that will break downstream coverage scoring. The chapter's modules must be designed to surface and address at least four critical misconceptions (unlike-terms-as-like-terms, n×n=2n, variable-as-fixed-unknown, coefficient-as-addition) through Math_Trap slides and IAS feedback flows, even though these cannot be formally linked to KB misconception IDs. The blueprint builder should flag each of these as KB insertion requests and use the Guided Discovery archetype (IAS 7, IAS 2) to surface them through carefully sequenced counterexamples — for instance, presenting 3x + 2y and asking students whether these can be combined, or showing n × n alongside 2n and asking which represents 'n squared'. The Indonesian curriculum context (Kurikulum Merdeka, ID geography) calls for scenarios drawn from everyday Indonesian life — market transactions, batik pattern sequences, warung pricing, school supply costs — which are culturally authentic anchors for LO sub-skill 5 and make the abstract modeling task meaningful before the symbolic machinery is fully in place.
